NASA Internship

In a team of 12 interns and under the guidance of a NASA engineer, we designed, built, and programmed two CubeSats as a proof-of-concept for wireless power transmission using collimated light (essentially a laser). The transmitting CubeSat pointed directly at the receiving side of the other CubeSat, then shot a beam of light towards it. A concave lens then diverged the light to spread across a solar panel to convert the light into energy. The real-world application of this is to provide power for satellites conducting missions on the dark side of the Earth, when they need more power than their solar panels can provide to run computationally expensive and energy-intensive programs, etc.

During the six weeks, we were expected to plan all the details and bring them to fruition by ourselves; however, we were provided with technical mentors to whom we could ask questions. I worked mostly on the software aspect of this mission. This included using inertial measurement unit (IMU) data, such as accelerometer and gyroscope readings, as well as GPS data to properly position the satellites so they would face each other while in orbit. For the fine-tuning of positioning for firing the laser, we used computer vision to identify the center of the four LEDs on the corners of the receiving satellite from a visual camera feed. Then, we coded the motors to move the reaction wheels accordingly, and then a magnetorquer dumped the excess angular momentum to stabilize the CubeSats.

Personal Projects

In middle school, I ideated and modeled the AeroHydroBottle, a water bottle that can convert humidity in the air to water without any external energy source other than sunlight. It uses a zinc-based iteration of a hydrophilic polymer network known as hydrogel to retain, purify, and condense moisture from the air to make drinking water. I submitted this innovation in various competitions and received accolades for my work.

Last year, I developed a prototype of the T-REX (Tunable Resonance Electricity eXperiment), a tile that generates electricity from mechanical and sonic energy by utilizing piezoelectric materials. My team and I created a working model and were honored to be named as Conrad Innovators in the Conrad Challenge. We are currently taking this product to the next level by conducting research with Professor Liling Huang at George Mason University as to how we can increase its efficiency. Creating these products helped me improve my research skills and creativity; I aim to apply this knowledge to my position as an intern at NASA.
